Sequenced by hits, not by the clock

Most step sequencers march to the transport. Stepper only moves when a drum tells it to — point it at your snare and every hit knocks the melody one step forward. Ghost notes can be filtered out, chance can thin the line, and the loop itself changes length as it plays.

Input

Trigger

Pick a target note — snare, kick, anything. Hits below the velocity threshold are ignored, so accents lead and ghosts sit out.

Input

Chance

A probability gate decides whether each hit lands. 100% marches in lockstep; 60% stumbles beautifully.

Melody

Steps

Sixteen pitches drawn as bars. Drag to write. The grid follows your drummer, not the grid lines.

Loop length

Length

A second sequencer sets how many steps each pass plays — 6, then 11, then whatever you draw next.

Loops that reshape themselves

Each time the melody completes a pass, the length sequencer hands it a new size for the next one. Draw 6 · 11 and the same sixteen notes never fall on the beat the same way twice — phrases rotate against the drums, resolve, and drift apart again. Drag the bracket on the length ruler to choose how many sizes take turns.

One sender, many listeners

Drop Sender on the drum track. It broadcasts every hit to any number of Steppers in the set — no input routing, no monitor juggling, and your tracks keep their keyboards. Give each listener its own target, chance, and lengths: one kit, an ensemble of melodies.
